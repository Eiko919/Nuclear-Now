The Physics

How Nuclear Energy Works

A single kilogram of uranium fuel releases as much energy as 3 million kilograms of coal — through a controlled, clean chain reaction.

01
⚛

Fission Event

A slow neutron strikes a U-235 nucleus, which splits into lighter nuclei, releasing 2–3 neutrons and ~200 MeV of energy per event.

02
🔗

Chain Reaction

Released neutrons split more nuclei. Control rods absorb excess neutrons, maintaining a steady, self-sustaining reaction rate.

03
💧

Heat Transfer

Fission heat is absorbed by a pressurised coolant loop. Multiple independent cooling circuits ensure complete thermal isolation from the turbines.

04
⚡

Electricity

Steam drives turbines at high efficiency. A 1 GW nuclear plant powers ~750,000 homes continuously — with zero combustion.

Myth vs Reality

Setting the Record Straight

Fear, not data, drives opposition to nuclear. Here are the facts.

❌
Myth
"Nuclear plants can explode like an atomic bomb."
Physically impossible. Reactor fuel is 3–5% enriched U-235. Weapons require 90%+. The geometry and material cannot achieve supercriticality.
❌
Myth
"Nuclear waste is an unsolvable, eternal problem."
All high-level waste ever produced globally would fill one football field 10 metres deep. Advanced reactors can reuse spent fuel, cutting volume 95%.
❌
Myth
"Nuclear causes more deaths than other energy sources."
Nuclear causes 0.03 deaths/TWh. Coal causes 24.6. Air pollution from fossil fuels kills 8 million people per year globally. Nuclear kills effectively none.
❌
Myth
"Chernobyl proved nuclear is too risky."
Chernobyl used an inherently flawed RBMK design with no containment dome. Modern Gen III/IV reactors have passive safety: they shut down safely without human action or external power.
❌
Myth
"Nuclear is too slow and expensive to matter."
South Korea and China build reactors on time and budget. Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) are factory-built in under 3 years. First-of-a-kind cost overruns don't predict mature fleet costs.
✅
Fact
Nuclear is the most land-efficient clean energy by a factor of 75–400×.
A single 1 GW nuclear plant occupies ~1 km² total footprint. The equivalent solar farm needs ~75 km². Wind needs ~400 km² of turbine-swept area. Minimal ecosystem disruption per TWh.

Nuclear Energy History

A 80-Year Journey

1942
Chicago Pile-1 — First Sustained Chain Reaction
Enrico Fermi achieves the world's first self-sustained nuclear chain reaction beneath the stands of Stagg Field, University of Chicago.
1951
EBR-1 — First Electricity from Fission
The Experimental Breeder Reactor in Idaho generates 200 watts — the first electric power from fission. Four light bulbs. The age of nuclear electricity begins.
1954
Obninsk — First Grid-Connected Nuclear Plant
The Soviet Union's Obninsk Nuclear Power Plant becomes the first to supply electricity to the national grid, generating 5 MW.
1958
Shippingport — First US Commercial Plant
Pennsylvania's Shippingport Atomic Power Station begins commercial operation, pioneering the pressurised water reactor design still dominant today.
1970s–80s
The Nuclear Boom
Hundreds of reactors commissioned worldwide. France launches a programme that would ultimately supply 75% of its electricity from nuclear — the highest share in the world.
1986
Chernobyl — and the Lessons Learned
The RBMK reactor disaster triggers global safety reviews. The root cause: a design without passive safety features and a culture of secrecy. All Western and modern designs are fundamentally different.
2000s+
Generation III+ and the SMR Revolution
AP1000, EPR, and VVER reactors enter service with passive cooling. Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) attract billions in investment from the US, UK, Canada, and beyond.
2022+
The Nuclear Renaissance
30+ countries announce new nuclear programmes. The EU classifies nuclear as sustainable. The US passes the ADVANCE Act. Nuclear's role in the clean energy transition is cemented.

Point Kinetics: Neutron flux follows dN/dt = [(k−1)/l*]·N + λ·C with one delayed group.

Doppler Effect: Hotter fuel broadens U-238 resonance absorption, reducing k. This negative feedback makes PWRs self-stabilising.

Delayed Neutrons: β=0.65% of neutrons arrive seconds later. Without them, the reactor period would be microseconds and uncontrollable.

Decay Heat: After SCRAM, fission product decay still generates ~7% rated power. Fukushima failed because this heat wasn't removed.

Thermal Balance: dT/dt = (P_fission − P_removed) / thermal_mass. Coolant removes heat proportional to ΔT and flow rate.
